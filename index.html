ScenarioPicture yourself in a scenario where your residence is struck by an earthquake, causing widespread panic. With less than two minutes to evacuate your apartment, what are the essential items you would gather before exiting in the midst of the chaos?
This scenario effectively highlights the significance of situational awareness in critical moments. Wondering how? First, let's grasp the true meaning of situational awareness.
So, what exactly does ‘Situation Awareness’ mean?
In the above mentioned scenario, we can observe that being conscious of the location of various items within the household, prioritizing essential items during an earthquake, efficiently gathering important belongings, choosing the safest route to exit the apartment, and successfully escaping the situation all contribute to gauging one's level of situational awareness and in-turn affect decision making.Let’s delve into the details!

World War IThe term Situation Awareness can be traced back to World War I where it was recognized as a crucial skill for crews in military aircraft.World War IIMilitary aviation during World War II. The term was first defined formally by Endsley in 1988. Identified having good SA as the decisive factor in air combat engagements—the "ace factor".

Theoretical ModelEndsley published her theoretical model for Situation Awareness. It was widely accepted since it significantly contributed to the understanding of cognitive processes.
Current stateSituational awareness continues to evolve with the rise of complex systems and digital interfaces. Fields such as healthcare, transportation, and emergency response integrated situational awareness principles into their practices.
The concept of situation awareness extended to everyday technology use, such as smartphones and smart homes. Research and applications expanded into diverse areas, emphasizing the importance of understanding one's surroundings in various contexts. Throughout this timeline, the evolution of situation awareness reflects a growing awareness of the cognitive processes involved in decision-making across different domains.

Any metric?Now, here’s the question, how do you measure how situationally aware you are? Say, a metric, if you wanted to be recruited for airforce, for example?

There are few measures and techniques which can be used to measure SA, and those techniques can be categorised broadly into these 5 classification:
Objective
Subjective
Performance
Process Indices
Multifaceted
Let’s look into these five in detail!
Objective measures directly assess SA by comparing an individual's perceptions to "ground truth" reality.

Participants are evaluated based on observer assessments, providing insight into their performance. They may be unaware they are being evaluated during the task. Questionnaires can assess SA based on their perception and comprehension during or after completing the task.

Few standard techniques for objective measures are:
SAGAT - Situation Awareness Global Assessment Technique
SPAM - Situation Presence Awareness Method
WOMBAT/HUPEX (More commonly used in aviation)

Objective measures
Subjective measures involve individuals rating their own or observed SA on a scale. This method is convenient as participants directly express their perception of their SA.

However, it's noted that individuals may overestimate their SA due to confidence or external influences, rendering this measurement subjective and potentially influenced by situational meta-awareness.

Few standard techniques for objective measures are:
SART
TLX

Performance measurement evaluates task execution effectiveness, considering time, accuracy, and outcomes. Faster completion may suggest better SA, but the correlation isn't definitive.

While performance-based measurement can be combined with other methods, it doesn't firmly link SA with task proficiency.

However, they may overlook participants' psychological states, leaving the question of "what is going on inside their head?" unanswered.

ProcessTo explain SAGAT, we conducted an experiment with our colleagues at the department.

Similarly to the question posed in the beginning, we set up a table with daily use items with some other items on the table.
We then covered the table with a cloth before the participants arrived.
We asked the participant to engage with the items once we reveal the instructions.
The instructions given out were:
You are in an earthquake.
b. You only have 5 seconds.
No other instruction was given, i.e. what to do with the items, what to pick, what not to, it was open to interpretation of the participant and how they use their SA in the given situation.
After they picked the items, they were asked to stop, and we covered the items with the cloth again.
Objective measurement/
observations.All the participants took very different items with them, few picked up the phone with charger, few without the charger
One person did not pick-up the phone saying they already had their phone with them during the experiment. So why pick someone else’s phone? Interesting that they could about this in 5 sec and react to it.
Almost everyone picked up a clothing item.
Conducting SAGATRight after the experiment ended, we asked them all to fill a small 2 min survey based on the experiment. This is where we bring in SAGAT.
The survey had 3 sections, to test their perception, comprehension, and projection.
The questions asked:
What items did you observe on the table (perception)
What did you pick from the table (comprehension)
Why did you pick those items? (projection)
How do we assess?Based on the responses from the participants, we graded their SA on a metric that we had created.
The metric basically was:
What were the items that they picked? (qualitative)
Number of items they picked (quantitative) (were they being too greedy / did they smartly pick the items)?
Did they show problem solving skills such as making combination of items that go together. (e.g, picking phone and charger together or picking non-perishable food item instead of perishable etc)?
Grading the reasons they stated for picking the items.
ResultsAfter all the participants are graded respectively, a finally scoreboard is read out, giving us the best situationally aware participant, at the same time, the worst too.
BriefSA is extremely important concept in critical fields such as aviation, military operations, and healthcare. SA is also used in general scenarios, you might not have even realised how commonly it is applied to even our everyday products like software applications!

Assessment techniques help measure how well SA is working, which helps with making decisions and reducing risks. These assessments include things like studying how people think and using simulations to check their understanding and it is an iterative process as it loops back into SA oriented designing.
Let’s take a look at few common applications
Military Control Systems Interfaces
Designers working on military command and control systems apply principles of SA in the design of interfaces to aid soldiers in understanding battlefield situations that are multi-faceted. The purpose of such an act is to make information more presentable and present support for commanders for making sound judgment. These interfaces are super important to make the soldiers work in an effective way, to respond quickly to dangers, and to ensure safety among all participants during their missions.
Air Traffic Management Systems
Air traffic controllers use SA to track airplanes in busy skies. Their computer screens show where airplanes are, their routes, the weather, and if there might be any problems. People check out how much the screens can help controllers understand what's going on and make decisions quickly, especially when they are very busy. They used tests called SAGAT to see if the screens are helpful during busy times.
Faculty Control Rooms
In industrial control rooms, workers use SA to observe how things are made, spot any problems, and prevent accidents from happening. The computer screens they use display information from sensors and alarms about the going on of machines. The screens are set to help them understand what's going on and quickly react if something goes wrong. Tests are used to find any problems with how the screens are set to make them better for the workers.
Smart Home Devices
The controls and screens at home on such devices as thermostats, security cameras, and lighting systems are telling the things that are happening at home. Such screens display things such as the temperature in the house, safety, and the number of energies that are being used up. With this information, people can make smart choices about their home, even if they're not there.

Linear Model vs. Nonlinear Nature:
Research suggests that SA is not always a linear, sequential process, as implied by Endsley's model.

Studies have found that individuals often engage in cyclic, iterative, or parallel processing, and the linear progression proposed by the model may oversimplify the actual cognitive processes involved in maintaining SA.
Failure to Capture Dynamic Aspects:
Empirical studies have indicated that real-world SA is dynamic and constantly evolving.

The Endsley model, with its distinct stages:
Perception, Comprehension and Projection might not fully capture the dynamic nature of SA in rapidly changing and complex environments.
Focus on Cognition Over Perception:
Critics argue that the Endsley model places a disproportionate emphasis on higher-level cognitive processes (comprehension and projection) and does not adequately address the foundational role of perception.

Perception is considered a critical component of SA, and its relationship with higher cognitive processes may be more complex than the model suggests.
Intrusiveness and Task Interference:
The interruption caused by SAGAT questions during an ongoing task may interfere with the participant's natural information processing and task performance.

This intrusion could potentially affect the accuracy of the SA assessment, as participants may not be able to fully focus on both the task and the assessment questions simultaneously.
Assumption of Perfect Recall:
SAGAT assumes that participants can accurately recall and report information about the environment after the interruption.

However, research has shown that memory is fallible, and there may be limitations to the accuracy of participants' retrospective reports, especially in dynamic and high-stress situations.
